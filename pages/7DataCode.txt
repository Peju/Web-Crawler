<p>An <b>orange</b> is a type of citrus <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> that people often eat or they can sniff the skin when grinded into a smooth powder. Oranges are a very good source of vitamins, especially <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">vitamin C</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Orange juice is an important part of many people's <a href="/wiki/Breakfast" title="Breakfast">breakfast</a>. The "sweet orange", which is the kind that are most often eaten today, grew first in <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> but now grows in many parts of the world.
</p><p>Oranges are round orange-coloured fruit that grow on a <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">tree</a> which can reach 10 metres (33Â ft) high. Orange trees have dark green shiny leaves and small white flowers with five <a href="/wiki/Petal" title="Petal">petals</a>. The flowers smell very sweet which attracts many <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a>.
</p><p>An orange has a tough shiny orange skin. Inside, the fruit is divided into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Segment" title="Segment">"segments"</a>, which have thin tough <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skins</a> that hold together many little sections with <a href="/wiki/Juice" title="Juice">juice</a> inside. There are usually ten segments in an orange, but sometimes there are more or less. Inside each segment of most types of orange there are <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a> called "pips". Orange trees can be grown from pips, but some types of orange trees can only be grown from "cuttings" (a piece cut off a tree and made to grow roots). The segments and the skin are separated by white stringy fibrous material called "pith".  In most types of oranges, the skin can be peeled off the pith, and the segments can be pulled apart with the fingers to be eaten. In some oranges it is hard to take the skin off.  Orange skin is often called "orange peel".
</p><p>Oranges are an important food source in many parts of the world for several reasons. They are a commonly available source of vitamin C. They last longer than many other fruits when they are stored. They are easy to <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> because each orange comes in its own tough skin which acts as a container. They can be piled into heaps or carried in bags, lunchboxes and shipping containers without being easily damaged.
</p><p>The colour <a href="/wiki/Orange" title="Orange">orange</a> takes its name from the fruit. The word "orange" is unusual because it is one of only a few <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> words that do not <a href="/wiki/Rhyme" title="Rhyme">rhyme</a> with anything.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" href="/w/index.php?title=Orange_(fruit)&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" title="Change section: History">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orange_(fruit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Change section: History">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sweet orange trees were brought to <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> from <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> in the <a href="/wiki/List_of_years" title="List of years">fifteenth century</a> (1400s). Before that time only sour oranges were grown in Italy. The name is from a <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> word, via Persian and Arabic. From <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, orange trees were taken to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, which all grow oranges for sale.
</p><p>There are several different types of sweet oranges. One of the most common types is called the "Valencia" orange, which comes from Spain and is also grown in Africa and Australia. It is one of the most important "commercial" oranges. (This means that it is grown for sale in shops.)
</p><p>One type of sweet orange is called the "blood orange" or "sanguine orange" (sanguine means <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> red). These oranges often have red marks on the skin, and some parts of the inside look as if they have blood in them. Some blood oranges make juice that is <a href="/wiki/Ruby" title="Ruby">ruby</a> red.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ambersweet_oranges.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2254" data-file-width="1999" decoding="async" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Ambersweet_oranges.jpg/200px-Ambersweet_oranges.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Ambersweet_oranges.jpg/300px-Ambersweet_oranges.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Ambersweet_oranges.jpg/400px-Ambersweet_oranges.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ambersweet_oranges.jpg" title="Agrandir"></a></div>These oranges are called "Ambersweets"</div></div></div>
<p>In the 1850s, in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, a tree growing in a <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a> garden was making very strange fruit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-npr-3">[3]</a></sup>  Inside each orange skin there was a large orange with no seeds.  At the bottom of the orange was a baby orange, which was really the bigger orange's <a href="/wiki/Twin" title="Twin">twin</a>. The little orange made a strange bump at the bottom of the orange skin, that looked just like a human <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navel" title="Navel">"belly-button"</a>. These oranges were named "Navel Oranges". They tasted very sweet, they had no seeds and they peeled quite easily. This made them a very good orange to grow <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commercially</a>. But they could not grow from seed. They could only grow from <a href="/wiki/Plant_cutting" title="Plant cutting">plant cutting</a>s.  Nowadays, thousands of these orange trees have been planted from cuttings. "Navel Oranges" are grown in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> and exported to many countries of the world. Every navel orange in the world has the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genes" title="Genes">genetic</a> make up as the oranges on that tree in the monastery in Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-npr-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mandarin_orange" title="Mandarin orange">Mandarins</a>, small flattened oranges with skins that come off easily, are believed to have come from <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Now there are several varieties. These include tangerines, which are redder than most mandarins, and clementines, which are large, smooth and plump. Mandarins of all sorts are very useful <a href="/wiki/Lunchbox" title="Lunchbox">lunchbox</a> fruit, because they are easy to peel and eat, but do not get squashed easily.
</p><p>Nowadays, many people of the world eat an orange or drink orange juice every day, because oranges are one of the best and cheapest sources of <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">Vitamin C</a>. Human bodies, unlike many other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animals" title="Animals">animals</a>, do not manufacture Vitamin C, so a human needs vitamin C in their diet regularly. (Vitamin C helps the body to grow, to heal wounds and fight infection.) Oranges are also a very good source of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dietary_fibre" title="Dietary fibre">dietary fibre</a>. But they do not contain high amounts of minerals. If a person eats an orange and a <a href="/wiki/Banana" title="Banana">banana</a> together, then they have had a very nourishing snack that supplies both <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>. Oranges are sweet and juicy.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditions">Traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" href="/w/index.php?title=Orange_(fruit)&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" title="Change section: Traditions">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orange_(fruit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Change section: Traditions">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moravian_Christingles.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="452" data-file-width="716" decoding="async" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Moravian_Christingles.jpg/220px-Moravian_Christingles.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Moravian_Christingles.jpg/330px-Moravian_Christingles.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Moravian_Christingles.jpg/440px-Moravian_Christingles.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moravian_Christingles.jpg" title="Agrandir"></a></div>Christingles prepared for a Moravian service</div></div></div>
<ul><li>Orange trees are a symbol of love and marriage in many cultures. Oranges are sometimes found in Renaissance paintings of married couples. One of the most famous is <a href="/wiki/Jan_van_Eyck" title="Jan van Eyck">Jan van Eyck</a>'s <i>"Wedding Portrait of the Arnolfini"</i>.</li>
<li>Brides traditionally wear orange blossoms in their hair or carry them in their bouquet at their <a href="/wiki/Wedding" title="Wedding">wedding</a>. Orange blossoms are often part of the decoration on a wedding <a href="/wiki/Cake" title="Cake">cake</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> was given a coronet of gold and enamel orange blossoms by her husband Albert. When their children were born, he had a jeweller add tiny green oranges to the coronet.</li>
<li>If an orange is peeled with a knife, it is possible to cut the peel off in one long unbroken piece. Schoolgirls in some countries chant a rhyme, and throw the long orange peel over their shoulder, then look at how it falls to find the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Initial&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Initial (not yet started)">initial</a> letter of the name of the boy that loves them.</li>
<li>In some countries, blood oranges are seen as a symbol of the death of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>.</li>
<li>In some European countries, a "Christingle" is a Christmas decoration using an orange and a candle to symbolise <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>' love for the world.</li>
<li>In cold countries, when fruit was scarce, an orange was often given at <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a>. In England, it was traditional to stuff the toe of a child's Christmas stocking with raisins, almonds and oranges.</li>
<li>Orange peel can be dried and treated with <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>. It is an ingredient in Christmas cakes.</li>
<li>Oranges are sometimes used to make a sweet-smelling pomander to perfume a room. This is done by sticking the stalks of <a href="/wiki/Clove" title="Clove">cloves</a> into an orange, and allowing the orange to dry out.</li>
<li>Orange <a href="/wiki/Jam" title="Jam">jam</a> is called <a href="/wiki/Marmalade" title="Marmalade">marmalade</a>. Traditionally, it is said to have been first made for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mary_Queen_of_Scots" title="Mary Queen of Scots">Mary Queen of Scots</a> or <a href="/wiki/Marie_Antoinette" title="Marie Antoinette">Marie Antoinette</a> when they were sick. Neither of these stories is true, as marmalade was made in Portugal for many years before either of them was born. The term "marmalade", originally meaning a <a href="/wiki/Quince" title="Quince">quince</a> jam ("marmelada" in Portuguese), derives from the Portuguese word for this fruit, marmelo.</li>
<li>An English children's song about the church bells of London begins with the line "'Oranges and Lemons' say the Bells of St. Clements..."</li></ul>
