<p><i><b>Citrus</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering</a> <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrubs</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rue" title="Rue">rue</a> family, <a href="/wiki/Rutaceae" title="Rutaceae">Rutaceae</a>. Plants in the genus produce <b>citrus fruits</b>, including important <a href="/wiki/Crop" title="Crop">crops</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Orange_(fruit)" title="Orange (fruit)">oranges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lemon" title="Lemon">lemons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grapefruit" title="Grapefruit">grapefruits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pomelo" title="Pomelo">pomelos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lime_(fruit)" title="Lime (fruit)">limes</a>.
</p><p>The most recent research indicates an origin in the Himalayas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Previous research indicated an origin in the part of <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> bordered by <a href="/wiki/Northeast_India" title="Northeast India">Northeast India</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Burma</a> (Myanmar), and the <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a> province of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and it is in this region that some commercial species such as oranges, mandarins, and lemons originated. Citrus fruit has been cultivated in an ever-widening area since ancient times.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Citrus plants are native to subtropical and tropical regions of Asia and the Malay Archipelago, and they were first domesticated in these areas. Some citrus species have been present in the Mediterranean basin for centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> This group of species has reached great importance in some of the Mediterranean countries, and in the case of orange, mandarin, and lemon trees, they found here soil and climatic conditions which allow them to achieve a high level of fruit quality, even better than in the regions from where they came.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The "native" oranges of Florida actually originated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_people" title="Spanish people">Spanish</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Conquistador" title="Conquistador">conquistadores</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The agronomists of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_Rome" title="Classical Rome">classical Rome</a> already made many references to the cultivation of citrus fruits within the limits of their empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> King Louis XIV of France housed citrus in orangeries, to protect the tropical fruit to be grown in the 1600s France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lance._1996_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lance._1996-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The generic name originated from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, where it referred to either the plant now known as <a href="/wiki/Citron" title="Citron">citron</a> (<i>C. medica</i>) or a conifer tree (<i><a href="/wiki/Thuja" title="Thuja">Thuja</a></i>). It is somehow related to the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">ancient Greek</a> word for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lebanon_Cedar" title="Lebanon Cedar">cedar</a>, κέδρος (<i>kédros</i>). This may be due to perceived similarities in the smell of citrus leaves and fruit with that of cedar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  Collectively, <i>Citrus</i> fruits and plants are also known by the <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance</a> <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanword</a> <i>agrumes</i> (literally "sour fruits").
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The large citrus fruit of today evolved originally from small, edible berries over millions of years. Citrus plants diverged from a common ancestor about 15 million years ago, which was about when it diverged from the closely related <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Severinia_(plant)" title="Severinia (plant)">severinia</a>, for example the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_box_orange" title="Chinese box orange">Chinese box orange</a>. About 7 million years ago, citrus plants diverged into two groups, the main citrus genus and the ancestors of the <a href="/wiki/Trifoliate_orange" title="Trifoliate orange">trifoliate orange</a> (poncirus), which is closely enough related that it can still be hybridized with all other citrus. These estimates are made using genetic mapping of plant <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A DNA study published in <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> in 2018 concludes that citrus trees originated in the foothills of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya">Himalayas</a>, in the area of <a href="/wiki/Assam" title="Assam">Assam</a> (<a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>), western <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a> (<a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>), and northern <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The three original species in the genus <i>Citrus</i> that have been hybridized into most modern commercial citrus fruit are the <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_orange" title="Mandarin orange">mandarin orange</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pomelo" title="Pomelo">pomelo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Citron" title="Citron">citron</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Within the last few thousand years, all common citrus fruits (sweet oranges, lemons, grapefruit, limes, and so on) were created by crossing those original species.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_record">Fossil record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fossil record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> leaf from the <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> of <a href="/wiki/Valdarno" title="Valdarno">Valdarno</a> (<a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>) is described as †<i>Citrus meletensis</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
In <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, fossil leaf specimens of  †<i>Citrus linczangensis</i> have been collected from coal-bearing strata of the Bangmai Formation in the Bangmai village, about 10 km northwest of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lincang_City" title="Lincang City">Lincang City</a>, Yunnan. The Bangmai Formation contains abundant fossil plants and is considered to be of late <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> age. <i>Citrus linczangensis</i> and <i>C. meletensis</i> share some important characters, such as an intramarginal vein, an entire margin, and an articulated and distinctly winged <a href="/wiki/Petiole_(botany)" title="Petiole (botany)">petiole</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>

(2014)</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Systematics" title="Systematics">systematics</a> of the genus are complex and the precise number of natural <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> is unclear, as many of the named species are <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a> clonally propagated through seeds (by <a href="/wiki/Apomixis" title="Apomixis">apomixis</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> evidence indicates that even some wild, true-breeding species are of hybrid origin.
</p><p>Most cultivated <i>Citrus</i> spp. seem to be <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">natural</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_speciation" title="Hybrid speciation">artificial hybrids</a> of a small number of core ancestral species, including the citron, pomelo, mandarin, and <a href="/wiki/Papeda_(citrus)" title="Papeda (citrus)">papeda</a> (see image).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Natural and cultivated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citrus_hybrid" title="Citrus hybrid">citrus hybrids</a> include commercially important <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> such as oranges, <a href="/wiki/Grapefruit" title="Grapefruit">grapefruit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lemon" title="Lemon">lemons</a>, limes, and some <a href="/wiki/Tangerine" title="Tangerine">tangerines</a>.
</p><p>Apart from these core citrus species,  <a href="/wiki/Australian_lime" title="Australian lime">Australian limes</a> and the recently discovered <a href="/wiki/Mangshanyegan" title="Mangshanyegan">mangshanyegan</a> are grown. <a href="/wiki/Kumquat" title="Kumquat">Kumquats</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Clymenia_(plant)" title="Clymenia (plant)">Clymenia</a></i> spp.  are now generally considered to belong within the genus <i>Citrus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AGLthesis_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-AGLthesis-18">[18]</a></sup> Trifoliate orange, which is often used as commercial <a href="/wiki/Rootstock" title="Rootstock">rootstock</a>, is an outgroup and may or may not be categorized as a citrus.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">Phylogenetic</a> analysis suggests the species of <i><a href="/wiki/Oxanthera" title="Oxanthera">Oxanthera</a></i> from <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a> should be transferred to the genus <i>Citrus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bayer_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-bayer-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Citrus_fruits.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1779" data-file-width="2700" decoding="async" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Citrus_fruits.jpg/220px-Citrus_fruits.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Citrus_fruits.jpg/330px-Citrus_fruits.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Citrus_fruits.jpg/440px-Citrus_fruits.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Citrus_fruits.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Slices of various citrus fruits</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tree">Tree</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tree">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These plants are large <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrubs</a> or small to moderate-sized trees, reaching 5–15 m (16–49 ft) tall, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spine_(botany)" title="Spine (botany)">spiny</a> shoots and alternately arranged <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a> <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> with an entire margin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> are solitary or in small <a href="/wiki/Corymb" title="Corymb">corymbs</a>, each flower 2–4 cm (0.79–1.57 in) diameter, with five (rarely four) white petals and numerous stamens; they are often very strongly scented.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fruit">Fruit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fruit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Hesperidium" title="Hesperidium">hesperidium</a>, a specialised berry, globose to elongated, 4–30 cm (1.6–11.8 in) long and 4–20 cm (1.6–7.9 in) diameter, with a leathery rind or "peel" called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pericarp" title="Pericarp">pericarp</a>. The outermost layer of the pericarp is an "exocarp" called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavedo" title="Flavedo">flavedo</a>, commonly referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Zest_(ingredient)" title="Zest (ingredient)">zest</a>. The middle layer of the pericarp is the mesocarp, which in citrus fruits consists of the white, spongy "albedo", or "pith".  The innermost layer of the pericarp is the endocarp. The space inside each segment is a <a href="/wiki/Locule" title="Locule">locule</a> filled with <a href="/wiki/Juice_vesicles" title="Juice vesicles">juice vesicles</a>, or "pulp". From the endocarp, string-like "hairs" extend into the locules, which provide nourishment to the fruit as it develops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Citrus fruits are notable for their fragrance, partly due to <a href="/wiki/Flavonoid" title="Flavonoid">flavonoids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limonoid" title="Limonoid">limonoids</a> (which in turn are <a href="/wiki/Terpene" title="Terpene">terpenes</a>) contained in the rind, and most are juice-laden. The juice contains a high quantity of <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid" title="Citric acid">citric acid</a> giving them their characteristic sharp flavour. The genus is commercially important as many species are cultivated for their fruit, which is eaten fresh, pressed for <a href="/wiki/Juice" title="Juice">juice</a>, or preserved in <a href="/wiki/Marmalade" title="Marmalade">marmalades</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pickling" title="Pickling">pickles</a>.
</p><p>They are also good sources of <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">vitamin C</a> and flavonoids. The content of vitamin C in the fruit depends on the species, variety, and mode of cultivation. Fruits produced with organic agriculture have been shown to contain more vitamin C than those produced with conventional agriculture in the <a href="/wiki/Algarve" title="Algarve">Algarve</a>, but results depended on the species and <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-23">[23]</a></sup>
The flavonoids include various <a href="/wiki/Flavanone" title="Flavanone">flavanones</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavone" title="Flavone">flavones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acidity_indicators">Acidity indicators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Acidity indicators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Moroccan professor Henri Chapot discovered that the acidity in the more common citrons or lemons is indicated by red on the inner coat of seeds specifically on the <a href="/wiki/Chalaza" title="Chalaza">chalazal</a> spot, violet pigmentation on the outer side of the flower blossom, and by the new buds that are reddish-purplish. The acid-free varieties of citrus are completely lacking the red color in all the mentioned spots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This designation was cited by <a href="/wiki/Herbert_John_Webber" title="Herbert John Webber">Herbert John Webber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leon_Dexter_Batchelor" title="Leon Dexter Batchelor">Leon Dexter Batchelor</a>, the editors of the fundamental treatise on citrus, namely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Citrus_Industry_(book)" title="The Citrus Industry (book)">The Citrus Industry</a>, which was published by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Riverside" title="University of California, Riverside">University of California, Riverside</a> in 1967.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cultivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Citrus_production" title="Citrus production">Citrus production</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lemon_on_a_Wood_Table.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4910" data-file-width="5730" decoding="async" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Lemon_on_a_Wood_Table.jpg/220px-Lemon_on_a_Wood_Table.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Lemon_on_a_Wood_Table.jpg/330px-Lemon_on_a_Wood_Table.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Lemon_on_a_Wood_Table.jpg/440px-Lemon_on_a_Wood_Table.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lemon_on_a_Wood_Table.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lemons are a citrus fruit native to Asia, but now common worldwide.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Limes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Limes.jpg/220px-Limes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Limes.jpg/330px-Limes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Limes.jpg/440px-Limes.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Limes.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lime_(fruit)" title="Lime (fruit)">Limes</a> in a grocery store</div></div></div>
<p>Citrus trees <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridise</a> very readily – depending on the <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> source, plants grown from a <a href="/wiki/Persian_lime" title="Persian lime">Persian lime</a>'s seeds can produce fruit similar to grapefruit. Thus, all commercial citrus cultivation uses trees produced by <a href="/wiki/Grafting" title="Grafting">grafting</a> the desired fruiting <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> onto <a href="/wiki/Citrus_rootstock" title="Citrus rootstock">rootstocks</a> selected for disease resistance and hardiness.
</p><p>The colour of citrus fruits only develops in climates with a (<a href="/wiki/Diurnal_temperature_variation" title="Diurnal temperature variation">diurnal</a>) cool winter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In tropical regions with no winter at all, citrus fruits remain green until maturity, hence the tropical "green oranges".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The Persian lime in particular is extremely sensitive to cool conditions, thus it is not usually exposed to cool enough conditions to develop a mature colour.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> If they are left in a cool place over winter, the fruits will change colour to yellow.
</p><p>The terms "ripe" and "mature" are usually used synonymously, but they mean different things. A mature fruit is one that has completed its growth phase. <a href="/wiki/Ripening" title="Ripening">Ripening</a> is the changes that occur within the fruit after it is mature to the beginning of decay. These changes usually involve starches converting to sugars, a decrease in acids, and a softening and change in the fruit's colour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ÖpikRolfe2005_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-ÖpikRolfe2005-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Citrus fruits are non<a href="/wiki/Climacteric_(botany)" title="Climacteric (botany)">climacteric</a> and respiration slowly declines and the production and release of ethylene is gradual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spiegel-RoyGoldschmidt1996_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel-RoyGoldschmidt1996-29">[29]</a></sup> The fruits do not go through a ripening process in the sense that they become "tree ripe". Some fruits, for example cherries, physically mature and then continue to ripen on the tree. Other fruits, such as pears, are picked when mature, but before they ripen, then continue to ripen off the tree. Citrus fruits pass from immaturity to maturity to overmaturity while still on the tree. Once they are separated from the tree, they do not increase in sweetness or continue to ripen. The only way change may happen after being picked is that they eventually start to decay.
</p><p>With oranges, colour cannot be used as an indicator of ripeness because sometimes the rinds turn orange long before the oranges are ready to eat. Tasting them is the only way to know whether or not they are ready to eat.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mandariner_Citrus_deliciosa.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Mandariner_Citrus_deliciosa.jpg/220px-Mandariner_Citrus_deliciosa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Mandariner_Citrus_deliciosa.jpg/330px-Mandariner_Citrus_deliciosa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Mandariner_Citrus_deliciosa.jpg/440px-Mandariner_Citrus_deliciosa.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mandariner_Citrus_deliciosa.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_Mandarin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mediterranean Mandarin (page does not exist)">Mediterranean Mandarin</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citrus_%C3%97deliciosa" title="Citrus ×deliciosa">Citrus ×deliciosa</a></i> plantation, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Son_Carri%C3%B3&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Son Carrió (page does not exist)">Son Carrió</a> (<a href="/wiki/Mallorca" title="Mallorca">Mallorca</a>)</div></div></div>
<p>Citrus trees are not generally <a href="/wiki/Frost" title="Frost">frost</a> hardy. Mandarin oranges (<i>C. reticulata</i>) tend to be the hardiest of the common <i>Citrus</i> species and can withstand short periods down to as cold as −10 °C (14 °F), but realistically temperatures not falling below −2 °C (28 °F) are required for successful cultivation. Tangerines, tangors and <a href="/wiki/Yuzu" title="Yuzu">yuzu</a> can be grown outside even in regions with more marked subfreezing temperatures in winter, although this may affect fruit quality. A few hardy hybrids can withstand temperatures well below freezing, but do not produce quality fruit. Lemons can be commercially grown in cooler-summer/moderate-winter, coastal Southern California, because sweetness is neither attained nor expected in retail lemon fruit. The related trifoliate orange (<i>C. trifoliata</i>) can survive below −20 °C (−4 °F); its fruit are astringent and inedible unless cooked, but a few better-tasting cultivars and hybrids have been developed (see <a href="/wiki/Citrange" title="Citrange">citranges</a>).
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Citrus_leaf.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1431" data-file-width="2004" decoding="async" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Citrus_leaf.JPG/220px-Citrus_leaf.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Citrus_leaf.JPG/330px-Citrus_leaf.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Citrus_leaf.JPG/440px-Citrus_leaf.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Citrus_leaf.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Leaf of <i>Citrus</i> tree</div></div></div>
<p>The trees thrive in a consistently sunny, humid environment with fertile soil and adequate rainfall or irrigation. Abandoned trees in valleys may suffer, yet survive, the dry summer of Central California's <a href="/wiki/Inner_Coast_Ranges" title="Inner Coast Ranges">Inner Coast Ranges</a>. At any age, citrus grows well enough with infrequent irrigation in partial shade, but the fruit crop is smaller. Being of tropical and subtropical origin, oranges, like all citrus, are broadleaved and evergreen. They do not drop leaves except when stressed.  The <a href="/wiki/Plant_stem" title="Plant stem">stems</a> of many varieties have large sharp <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spine_(botany)" title="Spine (botany)">thorns</a>. The trees flower in the spring, and fruit is set shortly afterward. Fruit begins to ripen in fall or early winter, depending on cultivar, and develops increasing sweetness afterward. Some cultivars of tangerines ripen by winter. Some, such as the grapefruit, may take up to 18 months to ripen.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hauptanbaugebiete-Zitrusfr%C3%BCchte.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="742" data-file-width="1603" decoding="async" height="102" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Hauptanbaugebiete-Zitrusfr%C3%BCchte.svg/220px-Hauptanbaugebiete-Zitrusfr%C3%BCchte.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Hauptanbaugebiete-Zitrusfr%C3%BCchte.svg/330px-Hauptanbaugebiete-Zitrusfr%C3%BCchte.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Hauptanbaugebiete-Zitrusfr%C3%BCchte.svg/440px-Hauptanbaugebiete-Zitrusfr%C3%BCchte.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hauptanbaugebiete-Zitrusfr%C3%BCchte.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Major producer regions</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Citrus_production" title="Citrus production">Citrus production</a></div>
<p>According to UN 2007 data, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> are the world's largest citrus-producing countries.
</p><p>Major commercial citrus-growing areas include southern China, the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean Basin</a> (including southern Spain), <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, the southern United States, Mexico, and parts of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. In the United States, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> are major producers, while smaller plantings are present in other <a href="/wiki/Sun_Belt" title="Sun Belt">Sun Belt</a> states and in <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_ornamental_plants">As ornamental plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: As ornamental plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orangerie_Kruidtuin_Leuven.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Orangerie_Kruidtuin_Leuven.jpg/220px-Orangerie_Kruidtuin_Leuven.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Orangerie_Kruidtuin_Leuven.jpg/330px-Orangerie_Kruidtuin_Leuven.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Orangerie_Kruidtuin_Leuven.jpg/440px-Orangerie_Kruidtuin_Leuven.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Orangerie_Kruidtuin_Leuven.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Orangery" title="Orangery">Orangery</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Botanical_Garden" title="Botanical Garden">Botanical Garden</a> in <a href="/wiki/Leuven" title="Leuven">Leuven</a> (<a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>)</div></div></div>
<p>Citrus trees grown in tubs and wintered under cover were a feature of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_gardens" title="History of gardens">Renaissance gardens</a>, once glass-making technology enabled sufficient expanses of clear glass to be produced. An <a href="/wiki/Orangery" title="Orangery">orangery</a> was a feature of royal and aristocratic residences through the 17th and 18th centuries. The <i>Orangerie</i> at the <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Palace of the Louvre</a>, 1617, inspired imitations that were not eclipsed until the development of the modern greenhouse in the 1840s. In the United States, the earliest surviving orangery is at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Airy,_Richmond_County,_Virginia" title="Mount Airy, Richmond County, Virginia">Tayloe House, Mount Airy, Virginia</a>. George Washington had an orangery at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Vernon" title="Mount Vernon">Mount Vernon</a>.
</p><p>Some modern hobbyists still grow dwarf citrus in containers or greenhouses in areas where the weather is too cold to grow it outdoors. Consistent climate, sufficient sunlight, and proper watering are crucial if the trees are to thrive and produce fruit. Compared to many of the usual "green shrubs", citrus trees better tolerate poor container care. For cooler winter areas, limes and lemons should not be grown, since they are more sensitive to winter cold than other citrus fruits. Hybrids with kumquats (× <i><a href="/wiki/Citrofortunella" title="Citrofortunella">Citrofortunella</a></i>) have good cold resistance. A citrus tree in a container may have to be repotted every 5 years or so, since the roots may form a thick "root-ball" on the bottom of the pot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lance._1996_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lance._1996-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pests_and_diseases">Pests and diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Pests and diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_citrus_diseases" title="List of citrus diseases">List of citrus diseases</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Citrus_canker_on_fruit.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="489" data-file-width="792" decoding="async" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Citrus_canker_on_fruit.jpg/220px-Citrus_canker_on_fruit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Citrus_canker_on_fruit.jpg/330px-Citrus_canker_on_fruit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Citrus_canker_on_fruit.jpg/440px-Citrus_canker_on_fruit.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Citrus_canker_on_fruit.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Citrus_canker" title="Citrus canker">Citrus canker</a> is caused by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gammaproteobacterium" title="Gammaproteobacterium">gammaproteobacterium</a> <i>Xanthomonas axonopodis</i></div></div></div>
<p>Citrus plants are very liable to infestation by <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">aphids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whitefly" title="Whitefly">whitefly</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scale_insect" title="Scale insect">scale insects</a> (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/California_red_scale" title="California red scale">California red scale</a>). Also rather important are the viral infections to which some of these <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ectoparasite" title="Ectoparasite">ectoparasites</a> serve as <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" title="Vector (epidemiology)">vectors</a> such as the aphid-transmitted <i><a href="/wiki/Citrus_tristeza_virus" title="Citrus tristeza virus">Citrus tristeza virus</a></i>, which when unchecked by proper methods of control is devastating to citrine plantations. The newest threat to citrus groves in the United States is the <a href="/wiki/Diaphorina_citri" title="Diaphorina citri">Asian citrus psyllid</a>.
</p><p>The Asian citrus psyllid is an aphid-like insect that feeds on the leaves and stems of citrus trees and other citrus-like plants. The real danger lies that the psyllid can carry a deadly, bacterial tree disease called <a class="external text" href="http://www.californiacitrusthreat.org/huanglongbing-citrus-greening.php" rel="nofollow">Huanglongbing (HLB)</a>, also known as citrus greening disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-californiacitrusthreat_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-californiacitrusthreat-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>In August 2005, citrus greening disease was discovered in the south Florida region around Homestead and Florida City.  The disease has since spread to every commercial citrus grove in Florida.  In 2004–2005, USDA statistics reported the total Florida citrus production to be 169.1 million boxes of fruit. The estimate for all Florida citrus production in the 2015–2016 season is 94.2 million boxes, a 44.3% drop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2008, the psyllid was spotted dangerously close to California – right across the international border in <a href="/wiki/Tijuana" title="Tijuana">Tijuana</a>, Mexico. Only a few months later, it was detected in San Diego and Imperial Counties, and has since spread to Riverside, San Bernardino, Orange, Los Angeles and Ventura Counties, sparking quarantines in those areas. The Asian citrus psyllid has also been intercepted coming into California in packages of fruit and plants, including citrus, ornamentals, herbs and bouquets of cut flowers, shipped from other states and countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-californiacitrusthreat_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-californiacitrusthreat-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>The foliage is also used as a food plant by the <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">Lepidoptera</a> (<a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterfly</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moth</a>) species such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geometridae" title="Geometridae">Geometridae</a> <a href="/wiki/Common_emerald" title="Common emerald">common emerald</a> (<i>Hemithea aestivaria</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Double-striped_pug" title="Double-striped pug">double-striped pug</a> (<i>Gymnoscelis rufifasciata</i>), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctiidae" title="Arctiidae">Arctiidae</a> <a href="/wiki/Giant_leopard_moth" title="Giant leopard moth">giant leopard moth</a> (<i>Hypercompe scribonia</i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Hypercompe_eridanus" title="Hypercompe eridanus">H. eridanus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hypercompe_icasia" title="Hypercompe icasia">H. icasia</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Hypercompe_indecisa" title="Hypercompe indecisa">H. indecisa</a></i>, many species in the family Papilionidae (swallowtail butterflies), and the <a href="/wiki/Black-lyre_leafroller_moth" title="Black-lyre leafroller moth">black-lyre leafroller moth</a> (<i>"Cnephasia" jactatana</i>), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tortrix_moth" title="Tortrix moth">tortrix moth</a>.
</p><p>Since 2000, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citrus_leafminer" title="Citrus leafminer">citrus leafminer</a> (<i>Phyllocnistis citrella</i>) has been a pest in California,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> boring meandering patterns through leaves.
</p><p>In eastern Australia, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bronze-orange_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bronze-orange bug (page does not exist)">bronze-orange bug</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Musgraveia_sulciventris" title="Musgraveia sulciventris">Musgraveia sulciventris</a></i>) can be a major pest of citrus trees, particularly grapefruit. In heavy infestations it can cause flower and fruit drop and general tree stress.
</p><p>European brown snails (<i><a href="/wiki/Cornu_aspersum" title="Cornu aspersum">Cornu aspersum</a></i>) can be a problem in California, though laying female <a href="/wiki/Khaki_Campbell" title="Khaki Campbell">Khaki Campbell</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Mallard" title="Mallard">mallard</a>-related <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">ducks</a> can be used for control.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Deficiency_diseases">Deficiency diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Deficiency diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Citrus plants can also develop a deficiency condition called <a href="/wiki/Chlorosis" title="Chlorosis">chlorosis</a>, characterized by yellowing leaves<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SumoGardener_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-SumoGardener-33">[33]</a></sup> highlighted by contrasting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaf_vein" title="Leaf vein">leaf veins</a>. The shriveling leaves eventually fall, and if the plant loses too many, it will slowly die. This condition is often caused by an excessively high <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline_soil" title="Alkaline soil">alkaline soil</a>), which prevents the plant from absorbing <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, or other nutrients it needs to produce <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll" title="Chlorophyll">chlorophyll</a>. This condition can be cured by adding an appropriate acidic fertilizer formulated for citrus, which can sometimes revive a plant to produce new leaves and even flower buds within a few weeks under optimum conditions. A soil which is too acidic can also cause problems; citrus prefers neutral soil (pH between 6 and 8). Citrus plants are also sensitive to excessive <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> in the soil. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_testing" title="Soil testing">Soil testing</a> may be necessary to properly diagnose nutrient-deficiency diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mauk_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mauk-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Culinary">Culinary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Culinary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Many citrus fruits, such as oranges, tangerines, grapefruits, and <a href="/wiki/Clementine" title="Clementine">clementines</a>, are generally eaten fresh. They are typically peeled and can be easily split into segments. Grapefruit is more commonly halved and eaten out of the skin with a spoon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>  Special spoons (<a href="/wiki/Grapefruit_spoon" title="Grapefruit spoon">grapefruit spoons</a>) with serrated tips are designed for this purpose. Orange and grapefruit juices are also  popular <a href="/wiki/Breakfast" title="Breakfast">breakfast</a> beverages. More acidic citrus, such as lemons and limes, are generally not eaten on their own. <a href="/wiki/Meyer_lemon" title="Meyer lemon">Meyer lemons</a> can be eaten out of hand with the fragrant skin; they are both sweet and sour. <a href="/wiki/Lemonade" title="Lemonade">Lemonade</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limeade" title="Limeade">limeade</a> are popular beverages prepared by diluting the juices of these fruits and adding sugar. Lemons and limes are also used as garnishes or in cooked dishes. Their juice is used as an ingredient in a variety of dishes; it can commonly be found in salad dressings and squeezed over cooked fish, meat, or vegetables.
</p><p>A variety of flavours can be derived from different parts and treatments of citrus fruits. The <a href="/wiki/Peel_(fruit)" title="Peel (fruit)">rind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Essential_oil" title="Essential oil">oil</a> of the fruit is generally very bitter, especially when cooked, so is often combined with sugar. The <a href="/wiki/Juice_vesicles" title="Juice vesicles">fruit pulp</a> can vary from sweet to extremely sour. <a href="/wiki/Marmalade" title="Marmalade">Marmalade</a>, a condiment derived from cooked orange and lemon, can be especially bitter, but is usually sweetened to cut the bitterness and produce a jam-like result. Lemon or lime is commonly used as a <a href="/wiki/Garnish_(food)" title="Garnish (food)">garnish</a> for water, soft drinks, or cocktails. Citrus juices, rinds, or slices are used in a variety of mixed drinks. The colourful outer skin of some citrus fruits, known as <a href="/wiki/Zest_(ingredient)" title="Zest (ingredient)">zest</a>, is used as a flavouring in cooking; the white inner portion of the peel, the pith, is usually avoided due to its bitterness. The zest of a citrus fruit, typically lemon or an orange, can also be soaked in water in a <a href="/wiki/Coffee_filter" title="Coffee filter">coffee filter</a>, and drunk.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical">Medical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Medical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Citrus fruits have well-documented nutritional and health benefits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> They can actually help prevent and cure some diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Citrus fruit intake has been associated with a 10% reduction in odds of developing breast cancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Oranges were historically used for their high content of vitamin C,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-23">[23]</a></sup> which prevents <a href="/wiki/Scurvy" title="Scurvy">scurvy</a>. Scurvy is caused by vitamin C deficiency, and can be prevented by having 10 mg of vitamin C a day. An early sign of scurvy is fatigue. If ignored, later symptoms are bleeding and bruising easily. British sailors were given a ration of citrus fruits on long voyages to prevent the onset of scurvy, hence the British nickname of <a href="/wiki/Limey" title="Limey">Limey</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Pectin" title="Pectin">Pectin</a> is a structural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heteropolysaccharide" title="Heteropolysaccharide">heteropolysaccharide</a> contained in the primary cell walls of plants. Limes and lemons, as well as oranges and grapefruits, are among the highest in this level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>After consumption, the peel is sometimes used as a facial cleanser.
</p><p>Before the development of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_fermentation" title="Industrial fermentation">fermentation</a>-based processes, lemons were the primary commercial source of <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid" title="Citric acid">citric acid</a>.
</p><p>Citrus fruit intake is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Association_(statistics)" title="Association (statistics)">associated</a> with a reduced risk of <a href="/wiki/Stomach_cancer" title="Stomach cancer">stomach cancer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid24011243_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid24011243-38">[38]</a></sup> Also, citrus fruit juices, such as orange, lime, and lemon, may be useful for lowering the risk of specific types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kidney_stone" title="Kidney stone">kidney stones</a>. Grapefruit is another fruit juice that can be used to lower blood pressure because it interferes with the metabolism of calcium channel blockers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
Lemons have the highest concentration of citrate of any citrus fruit, and daily consumption of <a href="/wiki/Lemonade" title="Lemonade">lemonade</a> has been shown to decrease the rate of kidney stone formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_effects">Health effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Health effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some <i>Citrus</i> species contain significant amounts of <a href="/wiki/Furanocoumarin" title="Furanocoumarin">furanocoumarins</a>, a diverse family of naturally occurring organic chemical compounds. In humans, some (not all) of these chemical compounds act as strong <a href="/wiki/Photosensitizer" title="Photosensitizer">photosensitizers</a> when applied topically to the skin, while other furanocoumarins interact with medications when taken orally. The latter is called the “grapefruit juice effect”, a common name for a related group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grapefruit-drug_interactions" title="Grapefruit-drug interactions">grapefruit-drug interactions</a>.
</p><p>Due to the photosensitizing effects of certain furanocoumarins, some <i>Citrus</i> species are known to cause <a href="/wiki/Phytophotodermatitis" title="Phytophotodermatitis">phytophotodermatitis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGovern_and_Barkley_2000_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-McGovern_and_Barkley_2000-41">[41]</a></sup> a potentially severe skin inflammation resulting from contact with a light-sensitizing botanical agent followed by exposure to <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> light. In <i>Citrus</i> species, the primary photosensitizing agent appears to be <a href="/wiki/Bergapten" title="Bergapten">bergapten</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dugrand-Judek_et_al._2015_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dugrand-Judek_et_al._2015-42">[42]</a></sup> a linear furanocoumarin derived from <a href="/wiki/Psoralen" title="Psoralen">psoralen</a>. This claim has been confirmed for lime<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nigg_et_al._1993_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nigg_et_al._1993-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wagner_et_al._2002_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wagner_et_al._2002-44">[44]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Bergamot_orange" title="Bergamot orange">bergamot</a>. In particular, <a href="/wiki/Bergamot_essential_oil" title="Bergamot essential oil">bergamot essential oil</a> has a higher concentration of bergapten (3000–3600 mg/kg) than any other <i>Citrus</i>-based essential oil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DFG_SKLM_report_2004_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-DFG_SKLM_report_2004-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>In general, three <i>Citrus</i> ancestral species (pomelos, citrons, and papedas) synthesize relatively high quantities of furanocoumarins, whereas a fourth ancestral species (mandarins) is practically devoid of these compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dugrand-Judek_et_al._2015_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dugrand-Judek_et_al._2015-42">[42]</a></sup> Since the production of furanocoumarins in plants is believed to be heritable, the descendants of mandarins (such as sweet oranges, tangerines, and other small mandarin hybrids) are expected to have low quantities of furanocoumarins, whereas other hybrids (such as limes, grapefruit, and sour oranges) are expected to have relatively high quantities of these compounds.
</p><p>In one comprehensive study of 61 <i>Citrus</i> varieties,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dugrand-Judek_et_al._2015_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dugrand-Judek_et_al._2015-42">[42]</a></sup> two papedas (<i><a href="/wiki/Citrus_micrantha" title="Citrus micrantha">Citrus micrantha</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citrus_hystrix" title="Citrus hystrix">Citrus hystrix</a></i>) had the highest concentrations of furanocoumarins of any <i>Citrus</i> species (even more than the bergamot), in both the peel and the pulp. The study also found high furanocoumarin content in the peel of lime and bergamot, and in the pulp of pomelo, grapefruit, and sour orange. These results are consistent with what is already known, that is, lime and bergamot lead to phytophotodermatitis, while pomelo and grapefruit are involved in grapefruit-drug interactions.
</p><p>In most <i>Citrus</i> species, the peel contains a greater diversity and a higher concentration of furanocoumarins than the pulp of the same fruit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nigg_et_al._1993_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nigg_et_al._1993-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wagner_et_al._2002_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wagner_et_al._2002-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dugrand-Judek_et_al._2015_42-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dugrand-Judek_et_al._2015-42">[42]</a></sup> An exception is <a href="/wiki/Bergamottin" title="Bergamottin">bergamottin</a>, a furanocoumarin implicated in grapefruit-drug interactions, which is more concentrated in the pulp of certain varieties of pomelo, grapefruit, and sour orange.
</p>
